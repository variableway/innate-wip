## The Two Camps

**Camp A: Ship it.** Get it out the door. Imperfect action beats perfect inaction. You can always iterate.

**Camp B: Get it right.** Quality is non-negotiable. Shipping something half-baked damages trust. Take the time.

Both are right. Both are wrong.

## Why It's a False Choice

The framing assumes **quality and speed trade off linearly**. But in practice, the relationship looks more like this:

- **Rushing without direction** produces garbage fast.
- **Polishing without purpose** produces beauty slowly (that nobody uses).
- **Clarity of intent** produces good things at whatever speed the problem requires.

## The Third Way: Clarity First

Before shipping or polishing, get clear on three things:

1. **Who is this for?** Not "everyone." A specific person with a specific problem.
2. **What does success look like?** Not "it works." A measurable outcome.
3. **What's the smallest version that proves the concept?** Not the MVP. The *minimum coherent experience*.

With those answers, the ship/polish decision makes itself:

- If the concept is unproven → ship rough, learn fast
- If the concept is proven → polish the experience, ship excellent

## Our Rule

> Ship when you're proud of what you've learned, not when you're proud of what you've made.

The pride in the making comes later, through iteration. But the learning has to start somewhere.
